What is the standard definition for "root cause" in flight punctuality reports?
09:15 · Ana Costa
HK
Based on Hi Fly's validated data governance documentation:

Root Cause is defined as "the primary, single-most contributing factor that directly initiated a delay or disruption event, traceable to a specific operational domain — Technical, Ground Handling, ATC, Weather, or Commercial."

Key rules to remember:
  · Only one root cause is assigned per event — the most proximate cause.
  · Contributing factors are logged separately in the secondary causes field.
  · IATA delay codes 00–99 map to root cause categories in Annex B of the Ops Manual.

📄 Source: Hi Fly Ops Data Dictionary v2.4 — validated by DataWings CoP, Mar 2026. Also in the Business Glossary.
⚠ This definition is reviewed quarterly. Flag discrepancies in the Knowledge Curation panel or contact João Cortiçadas (SME).

Business Glossary

DataWings CoP · Shared definitions aligned with COBIT APO14.02 · Eliminates cross-departmental inconsistencies

D
Delay — Flight Delay Operational
A flight is classified as delayed when it departs 15 minutes or more after the scheduled departure time (STD), as recorded in the OCC system. This threshold replaces previous departmental variations (10 min in Operations; 14 min in Finance) and serves as the single standard across Hi Fly.
Example: Flight HF402 STD 08:00 · ATD 08:16 → classified as delayed (16 min). ATD 08:14 → classified as on-time.

R
Root Cause (Flight Punctuality) Operational
The primary, single-most contributing factor that directly initiated a delay or disruption event, traceable to a specific operational domain: Technical, Ground Handling, ATC, Weather, or Commercial. Only one root cause is assigned per event — the most proximate cause. Contributing factors are recorded separately in the secondary causes field.
IATA delay codes 00–99 map to root cause categories per Annex B of the Hi Fly Ops Manual v2.4.

Wet Lease (ACMI) Operational
An arrangement in which Hi Fly provides an aircraft together with crew, maintenance, and insurance (ACMI) to another airline on a temporary basis. The receiving airline typically provides fuel and pays operational costs, while Hi Fly retains operational control of the aircraft and crew.
